Prediction markets are the most genuinely novel item on the menu of sweepstakes-casino alternatives, and also the least settled. The federal angle makes them structurally different from everything else in this directory. Here's how they actually work.

## The basic mechanic

A prediction market lets you **trade contracts tied to the outcome of a future event.** The classic form is a binary contract: "Will event X happen, yes or no?" You buy a "yes" or "no" position, and if you're right when the event resolves, your contract pays out a fixed amount. If you're wrong, it expires worthless.

The price of a contract moves with demand and reflects the market's collective estimate of how likely the outcome is. A contract trading at 70 cents implies the market thinks there's roughly a 70% chance the event happens. In that sense, prediction markets are as much an information tool, aggregating crowd estimates of probability, as they are a place to put money at stake.

## Why the regulator is different, and why that matters

This is the single most important thing to understand about prediction markets as an alternative.

Casino gambling and sweepstakes casinos are regulated (or banned) under **state** law. Prediction markets of the kind we're discussing are regulated under **federal** law, by the **Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC)**, the same agency that oversees commodities and derivatives markets. The contracts are treated as **event contracts** / derivatives, not as casino games.

That federal framing has two consequences:

1. **The state sweepstakes bans don't directly apply.** California's AB 831 and its peers target a state-law gambling structure. A federally-regulated event contract is a different animal under a different body of law, so those specific bans don't reach it.
2. **A federal framework can, in principle, mean broad reach.** Rather than fighting 50 state battles, a CFTC-regulated platform operates under one federal regime, which is part of why this model is attracting attention.

## Why we still rate it medium risk

If the federal angle sounds like a clean escape hatch, here's the cold water.

**The regulatory treatment of event contracts is itself actively contested.** Whether certain event contracts, especially those that look a lot like sports or entertainment betting, should be allowed under the CFTC framework, or whether they amount to gambling that states can regulate, is a live legal fight. There have been high-profile disputes between platforms, the CFTC, and individual states over exactly where the line sits.

On top of that, the consumer products built on prediction markets are new, and their **state-by-state availability is often unconfirmed.** We're not currently tracking a prediction-market platform in our directory — we'll add one once a credible product publishes clear state availability.

So we rate prediction-market platforms **medium** risk: the underlying legal theory is real and federally grounded, but the edges are unsettled and availability is unproven. That's a different kind of risk than a [single-currency](/guides/single-currency-model) platform faces, but it's risk nonetheless.

## How the experience compares

Prediction markets feel different from casino play and different from [DFS](/guides/dfs-vs-casino), too. You're taking positions on real-world outcomes, elections, economic indicators, sports results, cultural events, rather than spinning a game or drafting a lineup. For people who enjoy following the news and forming opinions about what will happen, that can be genuinely engaging. For someone who wanted slots, it's a very different activity.

It also rewards a different skill: reasoning about probability and real-world events, rather than game mechanics.

## What to check before you participate

- **Is it actually available to you?** This is the big one for prediction markets right now. Confirm the platform serves your state before assuming access.
- **Understand it's a markets product.** Onboarding looks more like opening a trading account than signing up for a casino, expect identity verification and a funded balance.
- **Follow the regulatory news.** Because the event-contract space is unsettled, the rules can shift. The "last verified" date on any prediction-market platform matters a lot.

## The bottom line

Prediction markets are the most intellectually interesting alternative in the post-ban landscape, and the CFTC grounding gives them a real legal foundation that the sweepstakes model never had. But "interesting and federally grounded" isn't the same as "settled and available to you today." Treat prediction-market platforms as a promising, medium-risk option to watch, and verify availability and current legal status carefully before putting money in.
