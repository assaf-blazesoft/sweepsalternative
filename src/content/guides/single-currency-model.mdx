When states banned sweepstakes casinos, they didn't ban "online games where you can win money." They banned something much more specific: a **dual-currency structure**. Understanding that distinction is the key to understanding why some platforms vanished overnight while others kept operating in the very same states.

## The two-currency trick

A traditional sweepstakes casino ran on two parallel currencies:

- **Gold Coins**, a play-money currency with no cash value, used to play games.
- **Sweeps Coins**, a promotional currency, usually given "free" alongside Gold Coin purchases, that *could* be redeemed for cash.

The whole legal argument rested on that second currency being free. Because you technically bought Gold Coins (no cash value) and merely *received* Sweeps Coins as a bonus, operators argued the cash-redeemable activity was a promotional sweepstakes, not gambling.

Regulators eventually rejected this. They looked past the two-currency structure to what was actually happening: people spending money, playing casino games, and cashing out winnings. The dual-currency design, in their view, was a wrapper around unlicensed gambling. So the bans were written to target that exact wrapper.

## What "single currency" changes

A single-currency platform removes the second currency entirely. There's **one** in-game currency, and there is no separate "free" redeemable sweeps coin running in parallel.

Why does that matter legally? Because laws like California's AB 831 are written *around the dual-currency mechanic specifically*. Remove the dual-currency structure, and the platform is, at least in theory, outside the scope of the statute. There's no Sweeps Coin to redeem, so the specific thing the law prohibits isn't present.

This is the legal basis on which platforms like [CardCrush](/review/cardcrush) and [Albumza](/review/albumza) continue to operate in California and New York after the bans.

## Why we don't call it "safe"

Here's the crucial caveat, and it's why we rate single-currency platforms **medium** risk rather than low.

**The label isn't the law.** A platform describing itself as "single currency" isn't automatically compliant. What matters is how the single currency actually functions:

- How do you acquire it?
- Can it be redeemed for cash, and if so, how?
- Does the practical experience still amount to wagering money on games of chance?

If a "single-currency" platform simply renames its mechanics while preserving the substance of casino gambling, a regulator can still come after it. The single-currency structure *helps*, but it isn't a magic shield. This is exactly why we couldn't yet confirm [Clash5](/review/clash5), it presents as single-currency, but we haven't verified how the model actually works, and we won't vouch for a label alone.

## Single currency vs parimutuel: a fair comparison

It's worth being clear about where single-currency platforms sit relative to the other low-risk options.

[Parimutuel gaming](/guides/parimutuel-explained) rests on **decades** of settled state and federal law. Its legal foundation isn't really in question, the only question is state availability.

Single-currency platforms, by contrast, are **newer**. They're responding to bans that are themselves only months old, and the legal theory hasn't been tested by years of enforcement and litigation the way parimutuel has. That doesn't make them illegitimate, it makes them **less proven**.

So if you want maximum legal durability, parimutuel is the safer pick. If you want something that *feels* closer to the casino-style experience you're used to and you're comfortable with a model that's newer, single-currency platforms are a reasonable choice, with appropriate caution.

## How to evaluate a single-currency platform

Since the model is newer, do a bit more diligence:

- **Read how the currency works.** A credible platform explains its model clearly. Vagueness is a red flag.
- **Check the redemption terms.** This is where the legal substance lives. Understand exactly how (and whether) winnings convert.
- **Confirm state availability.** Just because a platform is single-currency doesn't mean it operates in your state. Check your [state page](/state/california).
- **Watch the "last verified" date.** Because these platforms are responding to fresh law, their status can shift. Recency of information matters.

## The bottom line

The number of currencies a platform uses turned out to be the whole legal ballgame. Dual-currency sweepstakes casinos got banned; single-currency platforms found room to keep operating. But "found room" isn't the same as "guaranteed safe." Treat single-currency platforms as a promising, medium-risk option, newer and less tested than [parimutuel](/guides/parimutuel-explained), but a genuine alternative for players who want something close to the old experience.

This guide is informational, not legal advice. Always confirm a platform's current legal status in your state before signing up.
